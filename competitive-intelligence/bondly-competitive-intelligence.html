Executive Summary

The market is won at the agent, not the app.

South African bond origination looks like a consumer-choice market — slick websites, calculators, "apply online in minutes." It mostly isn't. The home-buyer rarely chooses their originator; the estate agent defaults it at offer-to-purchase, and the incumbents have spent a decade buying the agent layer. That single structural fact reframes every recommendation in this report.

How bond origination actually works

A bond originator is a free-to-the-consumer broker. The buyer (or, usually, their estate agent) submits one application; the originator packages it to each bank's spec and fires it at multiple lenders simultaneously — the big-five banks (Absa, FNB, Nedbank, Standard Bank, Investec) plus specialists (RMB, Sentinel Homes, HIP, SA Home Loans). The banks return competing quotes; the buyer picks one; the winning bank pays the originator a commission. The buyer pays nothing.

This model — verified 3-0 across both incumbents and SA's industry regulator — is the universal mechanic. Every player runs it. Differentiation therefore comes from distribution, speed, trust and segment, not the core mechanism.

Flow One application → many banks
  • Buyer/agent submits a single application
  • Originator tailors & submits to up to 9 lenders
  • Banks compete; buyer chooses the best offer
  • Winning bank pays commission on the settled loan
  • Buyer pays R0
Indicatively 1–1.9% of the loan — but no primary source discloses it.
Three research rounds confirm the bank-pays model (3-0) yet never the rate. The 1–1.9% band — ≈R12k–R23k on a R1.2m bond, implying a ~R3–6bn annual origination-commission pool — appears only in secondary snapshots. Treat as indicative; early-settlement clawback terms remain undocumented.
Why the contraction matters to Bondly
Incumbents are defending
A −14% purchase market means BetterBond/ooba are fighting over a shrinking pie — capital goes to retention, not innovation.
Switching is counter-cyclical
When new purchases fall, refinancing/switching rises as owners chase rate relief. Bondly's wedge is timed with the cycle.
R1.88tn of installed base
Every existing bond is a switch candidate. The switch TAM is the whole book, not just this year's R337bn of new loans.

02 · Who really chooses the originator

The contrarian question, answered.

This is the report's spine. If buyers choose originators, a better app wins. If agents choose for them, the war is fought a layer up — and the incumbents already own that layer.

"[Estate-agent referral is] the primary channel through which consumers are informed of their ability to use the services of bond brokers."
— MORCSA (Mortgage Originators Regulatory Council of SA), submission to the SA Parliament (pmg.org.za). Primary source.

SA's own originator regulator confirms it: this is a B2B2C referral market. The agent, at the offer-to-purchase moment, routes the buyer to whichever originator they default to. Referral relationships between agencies, agents, developers and bond brokers are described as "common place in the industry."

But — the crack — "a referral does not legally oblige a consumer to use a given originator."
The agent defaults the choice; they cannot compel it. A buyer who already knows Bondly can override the default.
Why this is a moat the incumbents bought

BetterHome Group didn't win the agent layer by persuasion — it acquired equity in it:

  • Stakes in RE/MAX SA (2015), RealNet, Chas Everitt, Tyson Properties (2015), Fine & Country (2022), Just Property (2024)
  • Majority shareholder of Private Property (2023) — ~3m monthly unique visitors, a captive top-of-funnel
  • amplifi — a social-media lead-gen engine sold to agents, partnered with Private Property

When your parent owns the agency, the portal and the agent's lead tool, the "referral" to BetterBond is structurally pre-loaded.

Source: betterhome.co.za/our-investments (primary, self-reported); propertyprofessional.co.za on amplifi. Rate limit prevented independent triple-verification — treat agency-stake list as single-source.
Strategic read Two doors are open — and Bondly should walk through both
Door A — go around the agent. The switch market has no estate agent. A homeowner refinancing an existing bond reaches Bondly direct. The incumbents' agency moat is worth nothing here.
Door B — win the agent. Where purchase origination matters, give agents a faster, higher-approval, better-tracked tool so they default to Bondly — competing on agent throughput, not consumer polish.
Corroborated three independent ways
MORCSA · the regulator
The originator industry body tells Parliament agent referral is "the primary channel" through which buyers reach originators — while conceding it never obliges the buyer.
The process map
Academic & portal descriptions agree: buyer signs the offer → agent asks how it's financed → forwards details to the originator → originator calls the buyer.
Industry reality · IOL
"Most agencies these days own, have JVs with, or are affiliated to" an originator — and historically earned 0.3–1.3% referral fees for the introduction.
The one number that didn't survive: a "~60% of home loans via origination" figure was refuted (1-2). The channel-split % remains unquantified — a known gap.

07 · How they became market leaders

First-mover, then integrate, then lock the channel.

The incumbents' dominance isn't a product story — it's a 25-year distribution story. The same playbook, in three moves.

1999 — Create the category
MortgageSA (later ooba) invents SA bond origination, persuading banks that a free-to-buyer broker is cheaper than branch acquisition. First-mover brand advantage compounds for two decades.
2000 — Fast follower at scale
BetterBond launches and races ooba for the same agent relationships, turning a two-horse category into a national footprint of consultants.
2015–2017 — Buy the agents
BetterHome takes equity stakes in RE/MAX, Chas Everitt, Tyson, RealNet — converting referral goodwill into ownership. ARC takes control of ooba (2016), adding capital firepower.
2022–2024 — Own the funnel
BetterHome takes majority of Private Property (2023), adds Fine & Country (2022), Just Property (2024), and launches amplifi to feed agents leads. The channel is now vertically owned.
2025–2026 — Fintech arrives
MortgageMarket secures direct bank contracts and embeds origination in EasyEquities, Takealot & telco apps — the first credible distribution innovation in 25 years.
The three repeatable moves
  • 1 · First-mover brand — own the category word ("bond originator") and the data narrative (Property Index).
  • 2 · Vertical integration — capture adjacent home-journey steps (portal, agency, insurance) so the lead never leaves the group.
  • 3 · Channel ownership — buy or lock the estate-agent layer where the default referral happens.
Bondly cannot out-incumbent the incumbents on this path — it's 25 years and a balance sheet behind.
The winning move is to change the game (switch + embed + transparency), not replay it.
The new playbook MortgageMarket revealed
Distribution by embedding (in apps people already use) + direct bank rails for speed. This is leapfrog, not catch-up — and it's available to Bondly too.

08 · Marketing & customer acquisition

Content for credibility, channel for conversion.

The incumbents win two ways: a deep SEO/content layer that earns trust and search traffic, and a captive referral channel that converts. Bondly must out-execute the first and route around the second.

What the incumbents do
  • Resource-hub SEO. ooba and BetterBond run vast libraries of calculators, guides and FAQs ("how do originators make money", affordability, pre-approval) that capture high-intent search and build authority.
  • Data-as-PR. ooba's quarterly Property Index / Oobarometer earns national press and backlinks — authority at near-zero marginal cost.
  • Captive lead-gen. Private Property (~3m monthly visitors) + amplifi feed agents, who feed BetterBond. The cheapest lead is one you already own.
  • Trust signals. Hellopeter 4.79/5 (2,322 reviews) is displayed as social proof and defends against challenger doubt.
How Bondly out-markets them
  • Own "switch" search. Incumbents under-serve refinance intent ("lower my home-loan rate", "switch bond"). Build the definitive switch content hub.
  • Launch a Switch Index. A quarterly data story on how much SA homeowners overpay — the ooba-Index playbook, aimed at refinancing.
  • Savings-led hook. Bondly's "Stop overpaying" calculator quantifies the prize before asking for anything — a sharper conversion mechanic than "apply now".
  • Embed, don't beg. Follow MortgageMarket: partner into apps/banks/employers where switchers already are.
Reviews are a feature spec, not a vanity metric.
Mine incumbents' 1–2★ reviews for the recurring failure themes (turnaround, consultant silence, wrong-bank submission) and make the opposite Bondly's product promise.

09 · Product, tech & the AI reality check

The most important slide for Bondly's roadmap.

Bondly's core bet is "AI automation beats the consultant model." The global evidence says: half-right. Automate the workflow, not the human out of the anxiety moment.

The digital baseline (table stakes)

Both incumbents already offer 24/7 online application, in-app document upload and e-signature. Digitisation is not a differentiator — it's the entry ticket. MortgageMarket raised the bar to <2-min pre-approval and <48-hr grants on proprietary, bank-direct rails. Bondly must at minimum match this speed.

The AI-automation reality, from abroad
  • Habito (UK) — pure-digital broker; CEO departed amid a hard pivot. Fully-automated broking struggled to convert.
  • Better.com (US) — aggressive no-human model; post-SPAC stock collapse; now publicly frames its AI as "empowering loan officers, not replacing them."
  • Trussle (UK) — Goldman-backed digital broker, absorbed by Better (~$9m, 2021) — independence didn't hold.
  • J.D. Power 2025 (US) — borrower satisfaction with lenders giving "useful guidance/advice" rose to 79% (from 69% in 2022). Buyers want more human guidance, not less.
The synthesis Hybrid is the only model that converts
A mortgage is a high-anxiety, once-a-decade, six-figure decision. Every market that tried to remove the human re-added one. The winning architecture:
  • AI does the grind — affordability pre-qual, document OCR, bank-matching, status chasing, turnaround compression.
  • Humans do the trust — the reassurance call at decline, the negotiation, the "is this the right call?" moment.
  • Bondly's edge: AI makes one human as productive as five, hitting incumbent reassurance at fintech cost.
Don't market "no humans". Market "instant answers, real people when it counts."
The data says the consultant is a conversion asset, not just a cost — use AI to scale it, not delete it.

10 · Weaknesses & regulatory levers

The attack surface.

Where the incumbents are soft, and where South African law hands Bondly a transparency weapon the incumbents would rather you didn't notice.

Incumbent weaknesses
  • Unverifiable superlatives. "25% of bonds", "largest", "leading" all failed adversarial verification (0-3). A challenger that only states what it can prove looks more honest.
  • The rate-claim trust gap. "Commission doesn't affect your interest rate" was refuted 0-3 — contested and unproven. It implies a possible incentive to steer to the highest-commission bank.
  • Purchase-only DNA. Switching is an afterthought across the field — an open flank.
  • The 1★ pattern, named. ooba's leading negative theme is consultant ghosting / post-submission silence — buyers go dark for weeks after documents are in (one verified case ran ~6.5 weeks unanswered). That is the exact failure an AI status-engine eliminates.
Regulatory perimeter
  • NCA s163(3). Originators are regulated under the National Credit Act 34 of 2005 — which already covers disclosure of fees/commission earned.
  • FSCA market conduct. Subject to the Financial Sector Regulation Act 2017 (eff. 1 Apr 2018) — not regulated as estate agents.
  • FAIS "advice". An automated affordability assessment or bank recommendation may constitute regulated advice, triggering suitability + record-keeping duties. Design the AI flow accordingly.
  • POPIA s71. Constrains automated decision-making with legal/material effect — a real design constraint on AI-only declines.
The law already requires commission disclosure. Bondly can simply lead with it.
"We show you exactly what each bank pays us — and we still recommend the best rate for you." Turns a compliance line into the brand's spine.
Compliance is also a moat-in-reverse: FAIS-advice and POPIA-automated-decision rules raise the cost of doing AI origination correctly. Building compliant-by-design AI is a barrier later entrants must also clear — make it a strength, not an afterthought.

11 · The Bondly playbook

Win the switch. Route the agent. Lead with the truth.

Five moves, ordered by leverage. Each plays where the incumbents are structurally weak, not where they are strong.

01 Own the switch market
It needs no estate agent, so the incumbents' moat is void. The TAM is the entire R1.88tn book, and it's counter-cyclical to the −14% purchase slump. Make "Stop overpaying" the category-defining switch brand before MortgageMarket pivots there.
02 Lead with radical transparency
Disclose commission openly (the NCA already requires it) and pledge best-rate, not best-commission. Directly attacks the incumbents' unproven "doesn't affect your rate" claim and the trust gap underneath it.
03 Hybrid AI, compliant by design
AI for instant pre-qual, OCR, bank-matching and turnaround; humans for the anxiety moments. Build FAIS/POPIA-aware automation now — speed of a fintech, reassurance of an incumbent, at a fraction of the cost-per-deal.
04 Distribute by embedding
Copy MortgageMarket's leapfrog: embed Bondly's switch flow into banks (as net-new switcher supply), employers, insurers and fintech apps. Routes around the agent channel entirely.
05 If you must do purchase origination — win the agent, not the buyer
Where you compete for new-purchase bonds, the customer is the agent. Give them instant pre-qual at the viewing, faster turnaround that closes their sale, and clean tracking — so they default to Bondly. Don't out-pretty the incumbents' consumer app; out-perform on the agent's throughput. But sequence it second — the switch market is the beachhead.
Two realities to execute against
⚠ The switch lane isn't empty
SA Home Loans already markets bond-switching with calculators and consultants — and ooba reframes "refinance" as equity-release, not rate-switching. The big two have left the lane open; SAHL hasn't. Bondly must out-execute SAHL on AI speed, savings clarity and UX — not merely claim an empty space.
Kill the switch friction in-product
The barrier is transfer + bond-registration cost. Solve it: capitalise the costs into the new bond (as SAHL does), surface Capitec's 50%-off registration and FNB paying cancellation/registration, and net it against savings — so the "should I switch?" maths is already done for the user.
